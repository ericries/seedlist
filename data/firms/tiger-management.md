## About

Tiger Management Corp. is an American hedge fund and family office founded in May 1980 by Julian Hart Robertson Jr. and Thorpe McKenzie with approximately $8.8 million in initial capital [^1] [^2]. McKenzie served as a founding general partner until 1982 [^2]. The firm is headquartered in New York City.

Robertson, born June 25, 1932, in Salisbury, North Carolina, earned a business degree from the University of North Carolina at Chapel Hill in 1955 and served two years in the U.S. Navy [^3]. He joined Kidder, Peabody & Co. in 1957 as a sales trainee and rose to become CEO of Webster Management Corp., the firm's asset-management subsidiary, by 1974 [^3]. After a sabbatical in New Zealand in 1978-1979, he returned to New York and launched Tiger Management at age 48 [^3].

Tiger Management grew into one of the largest and most successful hedge funds in history. By 1996, assets had reached $7.2 billion; by 1997, with $10.5 billion in AUM, it was the second-largest hedge fund in the world [^1]. Assets peaked at approximately $22 billion in mid-1998 [^1] [^2]. From May 1980 to August 1998, the fund delivered a compound annual return of 31.7% after fees, compared to 12.7% for the S&P 500 over the same period [^1] [^2].

The fund incurred significant losses during the 1998 Russian financial crisis, losing an estimated $600 million from ruble-denominated debt holdings [^2]. A wrong-way yen bet in 1998 erased approximately $2 billion in earlier gains [^1]. Robertson's refusal to chase dot-com era technology stocks -- he was reportedly "berated for 15 minutes" at shareholder meetings for his skepticism of internet valuations in 1999 [^4] -- led to nearly 20% losses in 1999 as the Nasdaq surged 85% [^2]. Investors withdrew approximately $7.7 billion, and Robertson closed the six Tiger funds on March 30, 2000, returning all outside capital [^2] [^3].

Following the closure, Tiger Management transitioned into a family office, retaining approximately $760 million in Robertson's personal assets [^2]. Robertson died on August 23, 2022, at age 90 from cardiac complications at his Manhattan home [^3]. The firm now operates as "Tiger 3.0" under the leadership of Alexander Robertson (Julian's son), who serves as president and COO [^5] [^6].

Tiger Management's post-2000 operation has two primary activities: (1) managing Robertson family wealth and foundation assets through direct public equity investments, and (2) seeding new hedge fund managers ("Tiger Seeds") in exchange for equity stakes in their management companies [^1] [^2]. As of September 2009, Robertson had helped launch 38 hedge funds through this seeding model [^1]. By approximately 2010, the seeded funds collectively managed roughly $22 billion [^7]. The firm typically provided seed capital of $25 million or more in exchange for 20-25% equity stakes in management companies [^8].

## Stated Thesis

Tiger Management's stated investment philosophy centered on fundamental, research-driven long/short equity. Robertson described his approach as: "Our mandate is to find the 200 best companies in the world and invest in them, and find the 200 worst companies in the world and go short on them. If the 200 best don't do better than the 200 worst, you should probably be in another business" [^9].

Robertson summarized his investment process as: "Smart idea, grounded on exhaustive research, followed by a big bet" [^9] [^10]. He emphasized management quality as the primary investment criterion, stating that "the main thing" was "getting good management and checking with their competitors, suppliers, and others to find out if they are good" [^9].

On risk management, Robertson stated: "Avoid big losses. That's the way to really make money over the years" [^9].

In his March 2000 closure letter to investors, Robertson wrote: "There is no point in subjecting our investors to risk in a market which I frankly do not understand" [^3] [^11].

For the post-2000 seeding operation, Tiger Management has described its mission as "sourcing and developing future leaders in the investment management industry" [^6].

**Note:** These stated principles apply to public equity hedge fund management and hedge fund seeding -- not to startup or venture capital investing, which Tiger Management does not engage in.

## Inferred Thesis

**Tiger Management is not a venture capital or startup investor.** Its investment activity falls into three categories, none of which involve direct startup investing:

**1. Public Equity Hedge Fund (1980-2000):** Tiger Management operated as a long/short equity hedge fund focused on publicly traded companies. Robertson employed deep fundamental analysis, on-site company visits, and management interviews to identify undervalued and overvalued public equities globally [^9] [^10]. The fund also made macro bets on currencies and bonds during the 1990s [^3].

**2. Family Office Public Equity (2000-present):** After closing to outside investors, Tiger Management continued to manage Robertson family assets in public markets. As of Q3 2024, the 13F-reported portfolio consisted entirely of Vanguard S&P 500 ETF (VOO) -- approximately $25.8 million in 43,200 shares [^12]. Historical 13F filings show positions in public companies such as CME Group, Goldman Sachs, Moody's Corp, Sherwin-Williams, Skyworks Solutions, and Verisk Analytics [^12]. No private or venture investments appear in public filings.

**3. Hedge Fund Seeding (2000-present):** Tiger Management's primary post-closure activity has been seeding new hedge fund managers. This involves providing capital (typically $25 million+) to launch new hedge funds in exchange for equity stakes (typically 20-25%) in the management companies [^8]. The seeded entities are hedge funds managing public equities, not venture capital funds investing in startups. Notable seeds include Ratan Capital Management ($25 million seed in 2009) [^2], Ananym Capital, and Sellaronda Global [^2].

**Startup/venture relevance:** Tiger Management's significance to the startup ecosystem is entirely indirect. The "Tiger Cubs" -- former Tiger Management employees and seeds who went on to found their own firms -- include several entities that became major startup investors: Tiger Global Management (Chase Coleman), Coatue Management (Philippe Laffont), and Pantera Capital (Dan Morehead) [^7] [^13]. However, these are independent firms, not Tiger Management investments. Tiger Management's own portfolio contains no verified startup or venture capital investments.
