## About

Social Capital, formerly known as Social+Capital Partnership, is a technology investment firm founded in 2011 by Chamath Palihapitiya [^1][^2]. The firm is headquartered at 120 Hawthorne Avenue in Palo Alto, California [^3].

Palihapitiya launched Social Capital after serving as a senior executive at Facebook from 2007 to 2011, where he led platform development and global growth initiatives [^1][^2]. Before Facebook, he held roles at the Mayfield Fund, AOL, and Winamp [^1].

Social Capital began as a traditional venture capital firm raising outside capital and investing in technology startups [^2]. By 2015, the firm reported managing more than $1.1 billion in capital [^4]. In 2018, Social Capital underwent a major restructuring: the firm stopped accepting outside investor capital and transitioned to deploying Palihapitiya's personal wealth as a technology holding company [^2][^5].

The firm currently manages two investment vehicles: the SC GP/LP Funds (legacy venture capital funds, closed to new investors) and proprietary capital deployed by Palihapitiya personally since 2019 [^1]. As of June 2025, Social Capital manages $2.147 billion from $1.4 billion in paid-in capital [^6].

In March 2024, Palihapitiya fired partners Jay Zaveri and Ravi Tanuku following an internal investigation by law firm Wachtell related to a special purpose vehicle formed around AI chip company Groq without Palihapitiya's authorization [^7][^8]. As of that date, Palihapitiya became the sole investing partner at Social Capital [^7].

Social Capital also became known for its SPAC (Special Purpose Acquisition Company) activity. Through the Social Capital Hedosophia series of SPACs, Palihapitiya took Virgin Galactic public in 2019, Opendoor and Clover Health public in 2020, and SoFi public in 2021 [^5][^9]. In September 2025, Palihapitiya launched American Exceptionalism Acquisition Corp. A, which raised $345 million targeting acquisitions in energy, AI, decentralized finance, and defense [^5].

In January 2024, Palihapitiya announced 8090, a self-funded incubator aimed at rebuilding enterprise software with AI tooling and offshore engineering teams, promising 80% of original features at 90% lower cost [^5].

## Stated Thesis

Social Capital publicly states its mission as "identifying emerging technology trends, partnering with entrepreneurs that are trying to solve some of the world's hardest problems and help them build substantial commercial and economic outcomes" [^1].

The firm describes four key sectors expected to create substantial markets over the next decade [^1]:

- **Deep Tech:** Commercializing frontier technology into sustainable businesses
- **Life Sciences:** Drug discovery shifting from traditional researchers to computer scientists
- **Energy Transition:** Innovation driven by global demand for sustainable energy
- **Creator Economy:** Disruption of media and content distribution

Palihapitiya has framed Social Capital's approach as aiming to "earn like Berkshire Hathaway and invest like the Red Cross" — pursuing financial returns while backing companies that solve problems that matter to society [^4].

In his 2024 annual letter, Palihapitiya emphasized partnering with "ambitious founders determined to solve hard and non-obvious problems" and described his approach as spanning early-stage to late-stage investments across private and public markets, focusing on technological and regulatory breakthroughs [^6].

## Inferred Thesis

Based on approximately 224 total investments with 13 unicorns, 7 IPOs, and 70 acquisitions (per Tracxn) [^10]:

**Evolution from VC to holding company:** Social Capital's most significant structural feature is its 2018 transition from a traditional fund model to a personal capital vehicle. This means Palihapitiya has no LP constraints on timing, sector, or stage — he can invest in anything from private startups to public equities, SPACs, and cryptocurrencies [^2][^5].

**Early portfolio (2011-2018):** The firm's venture capital period produced its most notable investments: Slack (invested early; Salesforce acquired for $27.7 billion in 2021), Yammer (backed in 2011; Microsoft acquired for $1.2 billion in 2012), Box (IPO in 2015), and Intercom [^4][^10]. The early portfolio was heavily concentrated in enterprise SaaS and collaboration tools.

**SPAC period (2019-2022):** Palihapitiya became the most prominent SPAC sponsor, taking Virgin Galactic, Opendoor, Clover Health, and SoFi public through blank-check vehicles [^5][^9]. Results were mixed: SoFi remained the only Chamath SPAC investment trading above its deSPAC price as of 2025, with median losses of approximately 75% across the other nine SPAC deals [^9].

**Current focus (2023-present):** The firm's recent investments reflect a shift toward deep tech, AI, energy, and the creator economy. Notable recent positions include Groq (AI inference hardware; raised $640M Series D at $2.8B valuation), Palmetto (clean energy platform; $1.2B in funding), and Beast Industries (MrBeast's creator-led business generating approximately $500M annual revenue) [^6].

**Solo operator model:** With Zaveri and Tanuku fired in March 2024, Palihapitiya is the sole investing partner, making Social Capital a de facto solo GP operation [^7].

## In Their Own Words

Chamath Palihapitiya on Social Capital's mission: Social Capital aims to "earn like Berkshire Hathaway and invest like the Red Cross" — pursuing financial returns while backing companies that solve problems that matter to society [^4].

Palihapitiya on the next decade: "The next decade of technology innovation will create multiple $10T markets" across emerging sectors including deep tech, life sciences, energy transition, and the creator economy [^1].

Palihapitiya in his 2024 annual letter: "Never give up. That's the secret" [^6].

On AI investment in 2024, Palihapitiya observed that venture capital allocated a record $150 billion to AI in 2024 but noted that "no killer apps emerged beyond chatbots" and that China reached parity with the US in foundational models by year-end [^6].
