## About

Revolution is an investment firm founded in April 2005 in Washington, D.C., by Steve Case (co-founder of America Online), Donn Davis (former AOL executive), and Tige Savage (former vice president at Time Warner Ventures) [^1][^2]. Case founded Revolution after leaving the AOL Time Warner board in 2005, following the 2000 AOL-Time Warner merger he had orchestrated [^2][^3].

The firm operates through three primary investment platforms [^4][^5]:

- **Revolution Growth** invests growth-stage capital of $25–50 million per deal in companies with product-market fit and approximately $20–30 million in revenue [^6][^7]. Revolution Growth has raised four funds: Fund I ($450M, 2011), Fund III ($525M, 2016), and Fund IV (~$500M target, closed 2022) [^7][^8][^9].
- **Revolution Ventures** makes early-stage investments of $1–12 million, with three funds totaling over $600 million: Fund I ($200M, 2013), Fund II, and Fund III ($215M, 2019) [^5][^10][^11].
- **Rise of the Rest Seed Fund** invests up to $1 million per deal in seed-stage startups located outside of Silicon Valley, New York, and Boston, with two funds of $150 million each (2017 and 2019) [^12][^13][^14].

Revolution also operates Revolution Places, a real estate and hospitality division managing properties including Exclusive Resorts and the Punta Cacique development in Costa Rica [^4].

As of 2025, Revolution has invested nearly $2 billion across more than 200 companies in 100+ cities across 40+ states, with 99% of capital deployed outside Silicon Valley [^3][^4]. The portfolio has produced 9 unicorns, 11 IPOs, and 75 acquisitions [^15].

## Stated Thesis

Revolution publicly describes its mission as backing entrepreneurs outside traditional coastal tech hubs who are "transforming legacy industries with innovative products and services" [^1][^4].

Steve Case has framed the firm's thesis around a geographic arbitrage opportunity, testifying before Congress in March 2025: "We all know that talent is everywhere, but opportunity is often not" [^16]. He has noted that when Revolution launched its Rise of the Rest Seed Fund in 2017, roughly 75% of venture capital flowed to just three states — California, Massachusetts, and New York — with 47 states fighting over the remaining 25% [^3][^16].

Case has described what he calls "The Third Wave" of the internet, stating: "We believe the next wave of really interesting, disruptive companies are going to have a policy aspect to them. That's because the sectors that are now starting to get reimagined get disrupted — like healthcare and education, food and agriculture, financial services. They all have a policy angle to them" [^3].

Revolution Growth specifically describes its investment approach as providing "speed-up capital" — helping companies expand from $100 million enterprises into billion-dollar companies [^7]. The firm publicly states it is "highly selective, making just three to four investments a year" at the growth stage [^8].

Revolution Ventures has stated its differentiated approach focuses on "fewer deals" with concentrated capital and significant ownership stakes, enabling deep operational involvement [^11]. Managing Partner Tige Savage has said: "We take a decidedly different approach from most early-stage investors who depend on a select few companies" to offset losses [^11].

## Inferred Thesis

Based on 30+ verified investments across Revolution's funds:

**Stage distribution:** Revolution operates a full-lifecycle model spanning seed through growth, but the majority of deployed capital is concentrated at growth stage through Revolution Growth's $25–50M checks [^6][^8]. Rise of the Rest provides seed-stage entry points (up to $1M) [^12], and Revolution Ventures covers early stage ($1–12M) [^5][^10]. This multi-fund structure allows the firm to invest across the entire startup lifecycle, though the funds operate with separate teams and strategies.

**Sector breakdown:** The portfolio skews heavily toward consumer-facing businesses and industries undergoing technology-driven disruption of legacy models. Based on verified investments: food and restaurant (Sweetgreen, CAVA, Revolution Foods), e-commerce and marketplaces (BigCommerce, StockX, CustomInk), sports and entertainment (DraftKings, Sportradar), healthcare and precision medicine (Tempus), defense and aerospace (Anduril, Hermeus), fintech and payments (Tala, Palla), logistics and returns (Optoro), and real estate technology (Homesnap) [^4][^15][^17][^18]. The firm has a notably strong concentration in food/restaurant companies and consumer brands relative to typical venture firms.

**Geographic concentration:** Revolution's defining characteristic is its non-coastal investment thesis. The firm claims 99% of capital is deployed outside Silicon Valley [^4]. Verified portfolio companies are headquartered across diverse U.S. cities: Washington, D.C. (Sweetgreen, Optoro, Homesnap), Chicago (Tempus), Detroit (StockX), Austin (BigCommerce), Boston (DraftKings), Costa Mesa, CA (Anduril), and numerous smaller cities through Rise of the Rest [^4][^15]. The D.C. metro area appears overrepresented relative to the "Rise of the Rest" branding, likely reflecting the firm's headquarters location and founding partners' networks.

**Founder profile patterns:** Revolution Growth targets companies with proven product-market fit and $20–30M in revenue [^6]. Steve Murray of Revolution Growth has stated the firm evaluates "the quality of the founding team, and particularly the founding CEO" and "why the person started the business, what is their vision" [^6]. The Rise of the Rest fund reports that 45% of its investments as of 2022 were in companies led by women or founders of color [^1].

**Co-investor patterns:** Rise of the Rest Seed Fund positions itself as a co-investor rather than lead, working alongside regional investors [^12]. At the growth stage, Revolution Growth has co-invested alongside SoftBank Capital and Telstra (BigCommerce), and other institutional growth investors [^19].

**Notable gaps:** Despite branding as a non-coastal investor, several of the firm's most prominent Growth investments (DraftKings in Boston, Anduril in Costa Mesa, BigCommerce which later moved to Austin) were in or near major tech markets. The "outside Silicon Valley" thesis is more strictly applied at the seed level through Rise of the Rest than at the growth stage.

"Bigcommerce is a big idea that aligns perfectly with Revolution Growth's philosophy: that technology can enable any entrepreneur, in any industry, located anywhere, to build a successful, high-growth business." — Steve Case, BigCommerce investment announcement (2013) [^20]

"At Revolution Growth, we look at a growth stage company as one that has product market fit, that maybe has 20 or 30 million dollars of revenue, and that is growing fast." — Steve Murray, Managing Partner, Revolution Growth, GrowthCap interview [^6]

"The quality of the founding team, and particularly the founding CEO... Why did the person start the business, what is their vision?" — Steve Murray on what Revolution Growth evaluates, GrowthCap interview [^6]

"We take a decidedly different approach from most early-stage investors who depend on a select few companies." — Tige Savage, Managing Partner, Revolution Ventures III announcement (2019) [^11]
