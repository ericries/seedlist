## About

Okta was co-founded in January 2009 by Todd McKinnon and Frederic Kerrest in San Francisco [^1][^2]. Both founders previously held senior roles at Salesforce — McKinnon as VP of Engineering leading a team of 300 developers, and Kerrest in business development [^2][^3]. The company was originally named SaaSure Inc. before rebranding to Okta in 2010 [^1].

McKinnon left Salesforce during the 2008 recession, convinced that cloud computing would transform enterprise software. He famously prepared a Google Slides presentation for his wife titled "Proposal to move to a new job starting a company (Why I'm not crazy)" [^3]. The founders initially envisioned cloud-based system management tools, but pivoted to identity management after discovering that prospects most urgently needed a way to manage employee access to cloud applications [^3].

Okta went public on the NASDAQ on April 7, 2017, under the ticker OKTA, pricing its IPO at $17 per share and raising approximately $187 million [^4][^5]. The stock opened at $23.51, well above the offering price [^5]. As of March 2026, Okta is a publicly traded company with over 19,000 customers [^6].

## What Investors Say

Andreessen Horowitz's investment in Okta's seed round was the first check ever written from the firm's inaugural fund [^7]. Floodgate (Mike Maples Jr.) joined as an investor in the Series B round alongside Greylock Partners and Khosla Ventures [^9][^10]. SV Angel (Ron Conway) invested in the Series F round in 2015 [^10][^14].

No independently sourced direct quotes from investors about Okta were found.

## What Founders Say

Todd McKinnon on the founding insight: "Being a part of Salesforce, seeing everything take off — AWS, Google Apps — it was clear to me there was going to be a cloud version of everything in the stack" [^3].

McKinnon and Kerrest on discovering the identity opportunity: "We looked around, we talked about it a bunch and we saw that identity was going to be a huge opportunity" [^3].

Frederic Kerrest on the early fundraising: "The first check that they wrote out of their first fund in the summer of 2009 was into our seed round" [^7]. He recalled the pressure: "When you're trying to build your first big technology company and you're sitting there trying to get $10-15 million to get going as a company. Very, very intimidating" [^7].

Kerrest on investor selection: "We tried to put folks around the table who were operators, who had the experience doing exactly what we were trying to do" [^7].
