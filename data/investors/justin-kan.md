## Background

Justin Kan was born on July 16, 1983, in Seattle, Washington, to a Chinese-American family [^1]. He attended Yale University from 2001 to 2005, graduating with a bachelor of arts double major in physics and philosophy [^1].

In 2005, Kan co-founded Kiko Software, an AJAX-based web calendar application, which was accepted into Y Combinator's inaugural summer batch [^1]. The company ultimately failed when Google released Google Calendar as a free competitor [^1].

In October 2006, Kan co-founded Justin.tv with Emmett Shear, Michael Seibel, and Kyle Vogt [^1]. The platform originated as a 24/7 "lifecasting" experiment where Kan documented his entire life through a head-mounted webcam [^2]. Justin.tv grew to 17 million monthly unique visitors by late 2010 [^1]. In 2011, the team spun off the gaming-focused section of Justin.tv into Twitch, which experienced explosive growth, reaching 100 million monthly viewers by the end of 2014 [^1]. Amazon acquired Twitch in August 2014 for $970 million [^2].

In 2011, Kan also launched Socialcam, a mobile video-sharing application that accumulated 16 million downloads before Autodesk acquired it for $60 million in July 2012 [^1]. In 2012, he co-founded Exec, an on-demand staffing platform, with his brother Daniel Kan; Handybook acquired it in January 2014 [^1].

Kan served as a partner at Y Combinator from March 2014 to March 2017, mentoring over 100 early-stage companies across multiple batches [^1] [^3]. Notable companies from his YC tenure include Zenefits, Ginkgo Bioworks, and Cruise [^1].

In 2017, Kan left Y Combinator to found Atrium, a technology-enabled law firm for startups [^4]. Atrium raised $75.5 million total, including a $65 million Series B led by Andreessen Horowitz in September 2018 [^4]. Atrium shut down in March 2020 after failing to achieve product-market fit [^4].

In 2020, Kan co-founded Goat Capital with Robin Chan, a seed-stage venture fund [^5]. In December 2021, he launched Fractal, a Solana-based marketplace for gaming NFTs, which raised $35 million from Paradigm and Multicoin Capital [^6]. Fractal was rebranded to Stash in 2024 [^1]. In November 2024, Kan co-founded Thin Ice Entertainment, a talent management company for music and creative industries [^1].

## Stated Thesis

Kan and Robin Chan launched Goat Capital as a "hybrid incubator versus a pure seed investment firm," writing checks ranging between roughly $500,000 and $3 million, with a target check of $1.75 million [^5] [^7]. The fund has stated interests in digital health, e-commerce, digital entertainment and gaming, robotics, and climate change [^5].

Kan has publicly described his investment approach as founder-first. On the 20VC podcast, he stated: "I think that the fundamental thing is that you want to invest in founders who are talented people" [^3]. He has emphasized founder determination over market thesis, stating his focus is on assessing whether "these people actually going to take this all the way" [^3].

Kan has articulated a preference for backing repeat founders who have learned from prior failures. He has publicly supported the idea that past failures can be the best education for a founder, as demonstrated by his investment in Rippling's Parker Conrad after Conrad's departure from Zenefits [^8].

On his own approach to startups, Kan has stated: "First time founders are obsessed with product. Second time founders are obsessed with distribution" [^9], though he later noted on Twitter that in retrospect the tweet "did not age super well" [^10].

Kan invests predominantly at seed and Series A stage, with occasional later-stage participation in high-conviction companies.

**Geographic concentration:** Portfolio is heavily concentrated in San Francisco and the Bay Area, with notable exceptions including Razorpay (India), Paystack (Nigeria), Xendit (Indonesia), and Ready Player Me (Estonia). Kan has meaningful international exposure in fintech payments, particularly in emerging markets, which is not emphasized in Goat Capital's stated thesis.

**Check size:** NFX Signal reports a range of $500,000 to $3,000,000 with a target of $1.75 million [^7]. Earlier angel investments (pre-Goat Capital) were likely smaller, in the $50,000-$500,000 range [^1].

**Founder profile patterns:** Kan gravitates toward technical founders solving problems they have personally experienced [^8]. He shows a willingness to back founders with controversial histories (e.g., Parker Conrad at Rippling after Zenefits) and repeat founders over first-time founders [^8].

**Co-investor patterns:** Frequent co-investors include Y Combinator alumni and partners (Michael Seibel), Andreessen Horowitz, and other founder-angel investors from the YC network [^7].

**Notable gaps vs. stated thesis:** Goat Capital publicly highlights robotics and climate as focus areas, but only 2 of 28 verified investments (7%) fall in those sectors. The portfolio skews more heavily toward fintech and developer tools than the stated thesis suggests. Consumer technology investments (Bird, OpenSea) exist but are marketplace/network-effect businesses rather than pure consumer apps [^8].

**Unicorns in portfolio:** At least 7 portfolio companies have reached unicorn status, including Mercury, Razorpay, Cruise, Ginkgo Bioworks, Scale AI, Rippling, and OpenSea [^11] [^12].

## In Their Own Words

"I think that the fundamental thing is that you want to invest in founders who are talented people." — Justin Kan, The Twenty Minute VC podcast [^3]

"Oftentimes I invested in things where the founders actually weren't that good. And it became apparent pretty quick within the first year." — Justin Kan, The Twenty Minute VC podcast [^3]

"It's very easy being a VC. That's the truth. Because you don't do any of the real work. I talk to founders all day and I give them my advice, and they either do it or they don't do it. But at the end of the day, you don't have any control." — Justin Kan, The Twenty Minute VC podcast [^3]

"First time founders are obsessed with product. Second time founders are obsessed with distribution." — Justin Kan, Twitter/X, November 2018 [^9]

"Adding more money to a situation of lack of product-market fit rarely works." — Justin Kan, reflecting on Atrium's closure, Twitter/X, 2021 [^4]

"The best entrepreneurs in Silicon Valley, the ones who create lasting companies and lasting value, they stick with their thing for DECADES." — Justin Kan, a16z Podcast [^17]

"If you burn out you aren't doing your customers or your investors or your employees any favors. You need to create a situation inside your company where you are going to be retained for a long time." — Justin Kan, a16z Podcast [^17]

"When you have nothing going for you except that you are willing to put in long hours, you should focus on market risk. Now as someone with abilities and skills, you should focus on execution risk." — Justin Kan, interview with Andrew Chen [^18]

"I've not convinced that raising a ton of money out of the gate is the right strategy. When you have a ton of money you spend a lot of money." — Justin Kan, interview with Andrew Chen [^18]

## What Founders Say

Matt Gamache-Asselin, co-founder of Alto Pharmacy, started the company in Justin Kan's living room. Kan later invested in and championed the company. Kan himself has publicly described Alto's journey: "Through sheer grit, Alto grew and made it out of that first pharmacy. But it wasn't easy. For Matt, the rollercoaster was always at the bottom. The company has been weeks away from cash out multiple times in its life" [^13].

No independently sourced founder testimonials found beyond Alto Pharmacy. Kan's role as a hands-on advisor has been noted in third-party analyses of his portfolio, with descriptions of him as "genuinely helpful to founders" who "helps founders think through product strategy, hiring, and fundraising" [^8], but these are not direct founder quotes.
