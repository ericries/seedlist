## Background

Howard Lindzon is a Canadian-born entrepreneur, angel investor, and author based in Scottsdale, Arizona [^1][^2]. He graduated from Forest Hill Collegiate Institute in Toronto in 1986, received a Bachelor of Commerce from the University of Western Ontario, and earned an MBA from Arizona State University (with additional studies at Thunderbird School of Global Management) [^1][^2].

Lindzon's career began inauspiciously: he was laid off from his first brokerage firm job during the October 1987 crash just months after starting [^1][^2]. He went back to school, then began managing a hedge fund in June 1998 [^1].

In 2006, Lindzon created Wallstrip, a daily financial satire video podcast that The New York Times described as "Squawk Box Meets Saturday Night Live" [^1][^2]. Wallstrip aired over 300 episodes before being acquired by CBS in May 2007 [^1][^2].

In 2008-2009, Lindzon co-founded StockTwits with Soren Macbeth, a social network for investors and traders that pioneered the "cashtag" (e.g., $AAPL) [^1][^2]. StockTwits grew to over 6 million monthly unique visitors and raised $30 million in 2021 [^2][^3]. In 2024, Lindzon returned as CEO of StockTwits [^2][^3]. Time magazine named StockTwits one of its "50 Best Websites" in 2010 [^1].

In 2009, Lindzon co-founded Social Leverage, a seed-stage venture capital firm, alongside Tom Peterson [^2][^3]. Social Leverage has deployed five funds, invested in 150+ startups, and most recently raised an $85 million Fund V [^3][^4]. The firm has seen 4 IPOs and 48 acquisitions across its portfolio [^3].

Lindzon's earlier investments include Rent.com (acquired by eBay in 2005 for $415 million), Golfnow.com (acquired by Comcast in 2008), and LifeLock [^1].

## Stated Thesis

Social Leverage describes itself as investing in "exceptional founders building the future of finance and vertical AI" [^3][^4]. Lindzon has stated the firm does "seed checks into financial companies and enterprise software up to $1M" [^5].

Lindzon has articulated his investment philosophy around several themes:

On founder selection: "A good founder overcomes a small market problem and has domain experience. Someone that understands the timing and commitment needed" [^5].

On the investor role: "A great investor should help in recruiting and fundraising for founders. We want to make sure we help them raise capital and recruit great people" [^5].

On identifying opportunities: He targets "financial services for investors, traders and the mass affluent" with emphasis on "non-obvious products, companies and founders" [^6].

Lindzon prefers being called "an investor in great businesses" rather than a "venture capitalist" [^7]. He has emphasized relationship-building: "Spotting talent and building relationships is becoming more important" [^5].
