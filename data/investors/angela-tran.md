## Background

Angela Tran is a General Partner at Version One Ventures, an early-stage venture capital firm based in Vancouver [^1]. She is a native Torontonian and naturalized US citizen based in the San Francisco Bay Area, investing across the US and Canada [^2].

Angela holds a PhD in Operations Research and Financial Engineering from the University of Toronto, where she also taught courses including "Entrepreneurship and Business for Engineers" and "Leadership and Leading for Groups and Organizations" [^3][^4]. After completing her PhD, she moved to Silicon Valley in 2010 and initially worked as a software engineer [^5].

She then co-founded Insight Data Science, a Y Combinator-backed startup that trained PhDs to transition from academic research to industry data science roles at companies like Facebook and Google through an intensive six-week program [^5][^6]. She operated Insight for approximately a year and a half before transitioning to venture capital [^5].

In September 2013, Angela joined Version One Ventures as the firm's first analyst, hired to manage day-to-day fund operations, identify entrepreneurs, and work with portfolio companies on data and hiring projects [^3]. She was promoted to Principal and led five investments, predominantly in healthcare and biotech [^1]. In September 2018, she was promoted to General Partner, becoming the firm's second GP alongside founder Boris Wertz after Version One had operated as a single-GP firm for over six years [^1].

Angela serves as a trustee on the board of the Computer History Museum, where she chairs the NextGen advisory committee, and is a board member of the C100 [^2]. She is also involved with the Creative Destruction Lab in Vancouver [^2].

## Stated Thesis

Angela and Version One publicly describe their focus as backing "mission-driven founders who are early in new areas" [^7]. Angela has stated that if a category already has branded terminology and a line of VCs waiting, Version One is probably too late [^7].

Angela's stated investment areas include deep tech (robotics, climate/energy, bio/health), AI/ML, developer tools, and enterprise SaaS [^2]. She has also developed a specific thesis around genomics and biosciences as a pathway to personalized medicine, focusing on companies making health data collection accessible and affordable [^8].

In an interview on the CIBC Innovation Banking podcast, Angela described her founder evaluation framework as centered on "Why you, why now?" — assessing the founder's unique qualifications to build the company and whether there is an immediate buyer need for the solution [^5]. She has stated that she prioritizes founders who have personally experienced the problem they are solving [^5].

On Version One's approach to support, Angela has emphasized that most founder support involves emotional and mental guidance during the building process, noting that "everything at the core of an organization is people" [^5].

## Inferred Thesis

Based on 16 verified investments where Angela's individual authorship or active deal lead is confirmed via Version One blog posts or press announcements (out of Version One's 113+ total portfolio companies [^9]), the following patterns emerge. Note: Angela and Boris share deal flow; this sample reflects investments where Angela's authorship is directly confirmed.

**Sector distribution (16 confirmed Angela-led investments):**
- Robotics/hardware/drones: 5 of 16 (31%) — Pickle Robot, Optimotive, TRIC Robotics, Arctus Aerospace, Pluto Mobility
- Climate/energy/batteries: 4 of 16 (25%) — Moment Energy, Bedrock Materials, Bolt.Earth, EnergyBank
- Bio/health: 3 of 16 (19%) — Gencove, Scanwell Health, Nia Health
- AI/enterprise SaaS: 2 of 16 (13%) — Ada CX, Antimatter
- Crypto/fintech: 2 of 16 (13%) — Lolli, Loon

**Stage distribution:** Predominantly seed, with some pre-seed investments. Version One's typical check is $500K–$750K with a target of $625K [^10].

**Geographic patterns:** Investments span the US and Canada, with a growing India allocation. Angela has backed 7 Version One portfolio companies in India and made 5 personal angel investments there since 2023 [^2]. India allocation appears to be an emerging strategic priority for the firm.

**Hardware bias in recent vintages:** A notable pattern in Angela's recent investments is a strong hardware component. In 2025, Version One invested $16M across nine new companies, with eight out of nine having a hardware component [^7]. Angela has articulated a framework for evaluating robotics opportunities: tasks humans simply can't do, or can't do at scale or for extended periods [^11].

**Deep tech differentiation:** While many seed funds focus on software-only companies, Angela's portfolio is notably heavy in physical-world technology (robotics, climate hardware, bio devices, drones, EVs). This diverges meaningfully from the typical seed fund's stated focus and aligns with her engineering PhD background.

**Co-investor patterns:** Frequent co-investors include Garage Capital (Optimotive, TRIC Robotics, Moment Energy), gradCapital (Pluto Mobility, Arctus Aerospace), Third Kind Venture Capital (Pickle Robot), and Trucks Venture Capital/Refactor Capital (Bedrock Materials).

**Founder profile:** Angela gravitates toward technically deep founders, often with advanced degrees or specialized domain expertise. TRIC Robotics' founder holds a PhD in robotics [^11]; Gencove's team works in computational genomics [^12]; Bedrock Materials was founded by researchers from Stanford and Tesla/Enovix [^20].

## In Their Own Words

On what she looks for in founders, from the CIBC Innovation Banking podcast: "We love backing mission driven founders, so people who won't stop at anything to solve that solve the problem that they've experienced firsthand." [^5]

On her investment philosophy, from her 10-year reflection blog post: "At the earliest stages, it is ALL about the founder and what drives their passion towards solving a specific pain point." [^25]

On the role of investors, from her 10-year reflection: "The best investors are not prescriptive, but can ask the right questions at the right time to help entrepreneurs find their own path." [^25]

On her approach to robotics investing, from the TRIC Robotics announcement: "It's not quite economical to automate everything (at least not yet). But there's still plenty of low-hanging fruit." She evaluates robotics opportunities by asking: "What are tasks humans simply can't do? What are tasks humans can't do at scale, or for extended periods of time?" [^11]

On market conditions, from the CIBC podcast: "The era of cheap money is over, but I think there's still access to, just money in general...there's just a higher bar that needs to be cleared." [^5]

On her climate investing motivation, regarding the Moment Energy investment: "At Version One, we've been focused on investing in companies tackling the climate crisis, and one way to do this is to reduce waste of what we already use." [^16]

On the "why now" for founders, from Enduring Planet: "The key here is to identify the customers who needed your product *yesterday*... The 'why now' can't be divorced from the customer." [^26]

On her Optimotive investment: "AI is driving major advancements in robotics, but turning these advances into practical products is still challenging. That's what makes Optimotive so exciting. Their innovations, like IRIS and the new MULE and SCRUBBLES robots, aren't just pushing boundaries—they're addressing real, immediate needs in industries like construction, renewable energy, building materials, and beyond." [^17]

On pattern recognition in VC, from her 10-year reflection: "I have to remind myself to continue to be open-minded and focused on what a venture can become if everything were to go right." [^25]

On building her network: "When I moved out to Silicon Valley in 2010, I didn't know anybody. So the first thing I did was went to my alma mater at the University of Toronto, and I said, 'Hey, what are there grads from engineering or arts and science or any department are here?'" [^5]
