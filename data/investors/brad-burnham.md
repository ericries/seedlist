## Background

Brad Burnham co-founded Union Square Ventures with Fred Wilson in 2003 [^1][^2]. He majored in political science at Wesleyan University [^1]. Burnham started working in information technology with AT&T in 1979 [^1][^3]. In 1989, he spun Echo Logic out of Bell Laboratories [^1][^3]. He joined AT&T Ventures in 1993, where he was responsible for 14 investments including Argon Networks, Audible, Avesta Technologies, Classic Sports Network, Multex Systems, Physicians Online, and Paytrust [^3][^4]. In 2001, Burnham co-founded TACODA, a behavioral targeting advertising company, before joining Fred Wilson to create Union Square Ventures [^1][^3].

At USV, Burnham is credited as the firm's strategic thinker. Fred Wilson has stated: "Brad is the strategist and the most principled investor in our firm. Brad is the person behind phrases like 'the application layer of the technology stack' and 'large networks of engaged users' that I use all the time. He gives me most of my good stuff which I often get credit for" [^5].

Burnham personally led USV's investments in Tumblr, Indeed, Meetup, Stack Overflow, and Duolingo [^3][^4]. He currently serves as a Venture Partner at USV, having transitioned from his earlier role as managing partner [^4][^6]. He is married with two children and lives in New York City [^1].

## Stated Thesis

Burnham was the architect of USV's original investment thesis: "Invest in large networks of engaged users, differentiated by user experience, and defensible through network effects" [^2][^5]. This thesis drove USV's earliest and most successful investments in Twitter, Etsy, Tumblr, and Meetup.

On USV's thesis-driven approach, Burnham has explained: "We don't really... we're not filter feeders... once we've achieved consensus, we go out and look for who is working in that area" [^7]. This distinguishes USV from deal-flow-driven firms — USV identifies market opportunities first, then seeks founders building in those areas.

Burnham has articulated the value of a thesis-driven portfolio: a focused thesis enables pattern recognition and peer support among portfolio companies without direct competition, helping USV manage a larger portfolio efficiently [^5].

His influential 2010 blog post "Web Services as Governments" framed platforms as governance structures, arguing that companies like Apple, Facebook, and Twitter should be evaluated by how they exercise power over their ecosystems [^1][^8].

More recently, Burnham has championed Web3 and decentralized data architectures. He has stated: "Web three is this new data architecture that's gonna bring fundamental change to the market" [^7]. He argues that Web3's real value lies in enabling users to own and benefit from their data, rather than platforms extracting value exclusively [^7].

## Inferred Thesis

Based on 12 verified personal investments and board seats, the following patterns emerge. Note: USV has made 500+ investments across 234 companies as a firm [^9], and Burnham's personal involvement represents a subset.

**Sector distribution (12 verified personal investments):**
- Networks/social: 3 of 12 (25%) — Tumblr, Meetup, 3Box Labs
- Marketplaces/platforms: 2 of 12 (17%) — Indeed, Tucows
- Developer tools/infrastructure: 2 of 12 (17%) — Stack Overflow, Yieldmo
- Education: 1 of 12 (8%) — Duolingo
- Media/advertising: 2 of 12 (17%) — SimulMedia, Blueprint Power
- Web3/crypto: 1 of 12 (8%) — Zerion
- Other: 1 of 12 (8%) — Path 101

**Stage distribution:** Burnham invests primarily at seed and Series A, consistent with USV's stated focus [^6]. His investment sweet spot is approximately $4.3M [^6].

**Geographic concentration:** Heavily concentrated in New York City, reflecting USV's headquarters [^1].

**Founder preferences:** Burnham has emphasized that the most important quality in founders has shifted over time. He values leadership ability — specifically "the ability to attract talent, and inspire that talent" — over pure management skills [^7].

**Notable pattern:** Burnham's personal investments show a strong tilt toward infrastructure and protocol-layer companies (Tucows, 3Box Labs, Zerion), more so than USV's overall portfolio, which includes more consumer-facing companies. This aligns with his role as the firm's infrastructure and protocol strategist.
