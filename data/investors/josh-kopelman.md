## Background

Josh Kopelman is an American entrepreneur, venture capitalist, and philanthropist who co-founded First Round Capital in 2004 [^1][^2]. He grew up in Great Neck, New York, the son of Dr. Richard Kopelman, a management professor at Baruch College, and Carol Kopelman, a real estate broker [^3].

Kopelman attended the Wharton School at the University of Pennsylvania, graduating cum laude in 1993 with a B.S. in Entrepreneurial Management and Marketing [^1][^2].

In 1992, during his sophomore year at Wharton, Kopelman co-founded Infonautics Corporation with Marvin Weinberger and Lawrence Husick [^3]. Infonautics pioneered online information services, offering its "Electric Library" subscription product to over 15,000 institutions [^3]. Kopelman took the company public on NASDAQ in 1996 at approximately $130 million valuation [^3]. He departed Infonautics in 1999 [^3].

In 1999, Kopelman founded Half.com, an online marketplace for used books, music, movies, and video games [^1][^2]. Half.com became one of the largest secondhand media sellers and was acquired by eBay in July 2000 for approximately $313 million in stock [^3]. After the acquisition, Kopelman remained at eBay for three years, running the Half.com business unit and growing eBay's Media marketplace to nearly half a billion dollars in annual gross merchandise sales [^2][^3].

In late 2003, Kopelman co-founded TurnTide Technologies, an anti-spam company that developed the world's first anti-spam router using TCP/IP packet analysis [^1][^3]. TurnTide was acquired by Symantec in July 2004 for $28 million in cash, just six months after founding [^3].

In 2004, Kopelman co-founded First Round Capital with Howard Morgan [^1][^4]. The firm began as a $7 million seed-stage fund and has since grown to manage over 10 flagship funds, with Fund X launched in September 2025 targeting $500 million [^4][^5]. First Round has invested in over 500 companies, producing 19 unicorns, 13 IPOs, and 206 acquisitions [^6].

Beyond investing, Kopelman served as chairman of the board of The Philadelphia Inquirer from 2016 to 2024, overseeing its transition to nonprofit ownership under the Lenfest Institute [^3]. He holds 16 U.S. patents related to internet technology [^3]. In June 2000, he was awarded Ernst & Young's "Entrepreneur of the Year" for the Greater Philadelphia region [^1].

## Stated Thesis

Kopelman publicly describes his focus as the first 24 months of a company's life: "when founders invent their product, hunt for customer love, figure out positioning, pricing, distribution, hire their key team, and solidify the company culture" [^2].

He frames his role as a supporter of founders rather than a strategist: "I'll tell you what I think, but recognize that I'm just one data point — and great companies are built by great founders, not great investors" [^2].

Kopelman has stated he values founder quality above all else: "Almost all of the focus goes towards the entrepreneur, rather than the product or business plan" [^7]. He looks for founders who are "nonconformists" and describes the best entrepreneurs as "cartographers who create their own map versus navigators of existing maps" [^7][^8].

On market opportunity, Kopelman has said: "I don't like to solve new needs. I like to solve urgent and pervasive needs but do it in a different way" [^9].

First Round Capital publicly states it invests at the "imagine if" stage. Kopelman has said: "The two most powerful words in humankind's history is 'imagine if.' We invest purely at that stage. There really is very little often in terms of proof points" [^10].

On ownership strategy, Kopelman has stated: "Our view is that it's pretty important to build it on first check. It just gets so expensive to accumulate ownership in the later rounds" [^11].

### Stage Distribution

Overwhelmingly seed-stage. First Round Capital's check sizes range from $500K to $4M, with Kopelman focusing on the earliest stage of company formation [^2][^12]. The firm's famous $510,000 investment in Uber (then "UberCab") in 2010 exemplifies the seed focus [^3]. Kopelman has expressed willingness to pay up for exceptional founders, having learned from passing on Twitter at a $5M pre-money valuation [^11].

### Geographic Concentration

Kopelman is based in Philadelphia, Pennsylvania, and First Round Capital maintains offices in both San Francisco and New York [^1][^12]. Portfolio companies are concentrated in San Francisco, New York, and Philadelphia, with some presence in other U.S. cities. The firm also invests in Canadian startups [^12].

### Founder Profile Patterns

Kopelman has a strong affinity for repeat founders and founders with prior operating experience. His relationship with Nat Turner illustrates this: Turner interned at First Round as a college freshman, founded InviteMedia (sold to Google for $81M in 2010), and then founded Flatiron Health with First Round's backing again [^13][^14]. The firm created a policy where successful portfolio founders automatically receive $250K and a desk for their next venture [^4].

### Co-investor Patterns

Frequent co-investors include Andreessen Horowitz, Google Ventures, Sequoia Capital, Accel, and Union Square Ventures. First Round often leads or co-leads seed rounds, with later rounds attracting major Series A/B firms [^6][^15].

### Check Size and Returns

First Round's check sizes range from $750K to $4M at seed stage [^12]. The firm's most notable return was Uber: a $510,000 seed investment in 2010 that yielded approximately $2.5 billion at the 2019 IPO [^3]. Other major exits include Flatiron Health (acquired by Roche for $1.9B in 2018), AppNexus (acquired by AT&T for $1.6B in 2018), and Ring (acquired by Amazon for $1B) [^3][^6].

### Notable Gaps

Despite Kopelman's stated focus on "the first 24 months," First Round Capital has evolved over 20+ years from a pure seed fund to one with significant follow-on capacity. Fund X targeting $500M [^5] is a far cry from the original $7M fund [^4], suggesting the firm's strategy has expanded beyond its original seed-only positioning. Kopelman's personal portfolio shows stronger enterprise/B2B concentration than the firm's public "sector-agnostic" messaging might suggest.

## In Their Own Words

**On the "imagine if" stage of investing:**
> "The two most powerful words in humankind's history is 'imagine if.' We invest purely at that stage. There really is very little often in terms of proof points." [^10]

**On his role as an investor:**
> "I'll tell you what I think, but recognize that I'm just one data point — and great companies are built by great founders, not great investors." [^2]

**On what he looks for in founders:**
> "Almost all of the focus goes towards the entrepreneur, rather than the product or business plan." [^7]

**On building ownership early:**
> "Our view is that it's pretty important to build it on first check. It just gets so expensive to accumulate ownership in the later rounds." [^11]

**On pricing as art and science:**
> "Valuation is both art and science. The science is picking a price, and the art is knowing which 5% of the deals to invest in at any price." [^11]

**On the importance of "the pick":**
> "A disproportionate amount of time is spent on execution rather than on the critical stage of idea selection." [^11]

**On market opportunities:**
> "I don't like to solve new needs. I like to solve urgent and pervasive needs but do it in a different way." [^9]

**On the investor ecosystem:**
> "I don't think that most successful VCs view themselves as stewards of capital. They see themselves as participants in the entrepreneurial ecosystem." [^8]

**On the first 24 months:**
> "The first 18 to 24 months are a magical time in a company's life." [^11]

**On team-based attribution:**
> "We've chosen to play the venture game as a team." [^11]

**On Notion's founding pitch:**
> "I remember walking out and thinking, 'This is different from any founder pitch I've ever taken.' There was no screenshot, no mockup. It was very conceptual, but I felt like I understood at the highest level what he wanted to do." [^15]

**On the Nat Turner relationship:**
> "I feel that I'm fortunate because although First Round is a venture firm, it's also a startup — it's an interesting company because we get to partner with the Nat Turners of the world and help them succeed." [^13]

## What Founders Say

**Dave Girouard, CEO of Upstart, on Kopelman's directness:**
During a board meeting, Kopelman asked Girouard: "You know, when First Round first invested, I remember you telling me there were going to be hordes at the gate for this product. I don't sense that's the case here — are there hordes at the gate, Dave?" Girouard reflected: "It was an admission that while we were showing numbers and progress, we were trying to talk around the fact that we hadn't quite figured it out yet." Girouard described Kopelman's feedback during Series B fundraising as "very helpful" though "very piercing" [^17].

**Nat Turner, co-founder of Flatiron Health and InviteMedia:**
Turner first met Kopelman in January 2005 as a Penn freshman seeking advice on a campus food delivery business. Kopelman told him: "I don't like your business, but I like you. Will you intern for us?" Turner took the internship, built First Round's first website, and later founded InviteMedia (sold to Google for $81M in 2010) with First Round as first investor. When Turner co-founded Flatiron Health, First Round co-led the $8M seed round alongside Google Ventures [^13][^14][^16].

**Ivan Zhao, co-founder of Notion:**
Zhao's pitch to Kopelman was unlike any other — instead of discussing competitors like Microsoft Word or Google Docs, they spent an hour diving into the open form factor of paper and how software could be similar. Kopelman was so impressed he wrote the largest check in Notion's $2 million seed round in 2013 [^15].

**Unnamed founder (via LinkedIn):**
"Working with Josh was one of the best parts of running my company and I would eagerly work with him again." [^18]

**Unnamed founder (via Technical.ly):**
A founder described Kopelman as "more than the lead investor and Board Member," serving as "a true partner and an inspiration," with "the ability to help you to see the opportunities and to help you to lead" [^8].
