## Background

Leif Danielsen is a General Partner at Acequia Capital (AceCap), an early-stage investment platform founded in 2010 and headquartered in Seattle [^1][^2]. Before entering venture capital, Danielsen worked at Amazon and at ChemPoint.com in operations management and data analysis roles [^3][^4]. He also worked at Dwellable, a vacation rental startup that was later acquired by HomeAway for $18 million in 2015 [^3][^5].

Danielsen studied Finance and Entrepreneurship at the University of Washington's Michael G. Foster School of Business (2010-2015) [^3]. He joined Acequia Capital as an Investor and Partner in 2015, was promoted to General Partner in 2021, and has since participated in over 350 investments across the US, Europe, and Southeast Asia [^1][^3][^6]. In December 2024, he co-founded The New Industrial Corporation (NIC), an investment company merging venture and infrastructure capital to support reindustrialization, where he serves as Co-Founder and Advisor [^3][^7].

## Stated Thesis

Danielsen publicly describes his focus as backing cross-border deep-tech and frontier-tech companies, with a particular emphasis on being personally accessible to founders rather than serving as a purely financial gatekeeper [^6][^8]. In a LinkedIn post announcing the New Industrial Corporation, Danielsen stated that Acequia Capital has "spent the last decade backing physical-world innovators," citing portfolio companies Formlabs, Tulip Interfaces, Atomic Industries, and Stoke Space as examples [^7]. He identified a key challenge in the industrial space: "You can't pour concrete with seed equity — CapEx eats ownership" when traditional venture timelines do not match industrial projects [^7].

More broadly, Acequia Capital positions itself as "a center of a global innovation ecosystem with unique capability to detect earliest signal in emerging technology trends" and focuses on partnering with technical founders building next-wave global businesses [^2][^9].

## Inferred Thesis

Based on the firm's portfolio of 175+ companies tracked on aggregator platforms (of the 350+ total claimed), Acequia Capital's actual investment behavior shows the following patterns [^9][^10]:

**Stage distribution:** The firm's investments heavily skew toward seed stage, with 89 seed investments, 17 Series A investments, and 4 Series B investments — making seed roughly 81% of tracked investments (89 of 110 stage-identified deals) [^9].

**Sector distribution:** Based on the portfolio page and aggregator data: Enterprise Software leads with 56 investments (approximately 32% of 175), followed by Consumer Tech at 27 (15%), High Tech at 27 (15%), Enterprise Infrastructure at 19 (11%), and Life Sciences at 11 (6%) [^9]. The firm's portfolio page also shows significant clusters in logistics, gaming/expression, AI/ML, and "New Industrials" (advanced manufacturing, space, energy, nuclear) [^11].

**Geographic distribution:** The portfolio spans 25+ countries, with the US accounting for the largest share (47 companies), followed by the UK (20), France (8), Germany (7), and Switzerland (5) [^9]. This cross-border footprint is unusually broad for a Seattle-based seed fund.

**Check size:** The firm's range is $100K to $5M, with a sweet spot at $1.5M [^6]. Average seed round size is $4.17M and average Series A round size is $16.8M [^9].

**Co-investor patterns:** Danielsen frequently co-invests alongside specialist funds like Point Nine Capital [^6]. The firm co-invested with Lowercarbon Capital and Wilbe Capital in Milvus Advanced, and with IA Ventures and Andreessen Horowitz in Uthana [^12][^13].

**Notable gap:** Despite positioning around "New Industrials" and deep-tech, the majority of tracked investments are in enterprise software and consumer tech. The industrial and life sciences investments, while prominent in marketing, represent a smaller share of the overall portfolio.

## In Their Own Words

In a LinkedIn post about the Sana acquisition by Workday, Danielsen described meeting founder Joel Hellermark approximately seven years earlier as "an ambitious young founder with a vision to reshape learning using the advanced machine learning models emerging from research labs," and said Hellermark's AI vision "led us not only to invest, but to reimagine what AI could do for people" [^15].

On the launch of the New Industrial Corporation, Danielsen wrote on LinkedIn: "At Acequia Capital, we've spent the last decade backing physical-world innovators" and identified the core financing challenge: "You can't pour concrete with seed equity — CapEx eats ownership" [^7].

Announcing Acequia Capital's approval by the New Mexico State Investment Council for a $15M commitment, Danielsen stated: "We're honored that our model and vision resonated with them. We're excited to partner with NMSIC as we continue investing in the founders rebuilding the technological and industrial backbone of the U.S." [^18].
