## Background

Shlomo Kramer is an Israeli technology entrepreneur and investor widely recognized as "the godfather of Israeli cybersecurity" [^1][^2]. He has co-founded three cybersecurity companies that each achieved multi-billion-dollar valuations:

**Check Point Software Technologies** (1993): Co-founded with Gil Shwed and Marius Nacht, Check Point introduced the first commercial firewall and went public in 1996. The company maintains approximately a $20 billion market capitalization [^1][^2][^3].

**Imperva** (2002, originally WebCohort, renamed 2004): Co-founded with Mickey Boodaei and Amichai Shulman, Imperva pioneered web application firewalls (WAF). The company went public in 2011 and was later acquired by Thoma Bravo [^1][^2][^3].

**Cato Networks** (2015): Co-founded with Gur Shatz, where Kramer currently serves as CEO. Cato created the SASE (Secure Access Service Edge) category and has achieved over $350 million in annual recurring revenue, with a valuation exceeding $3 billion [^1][^2][^4].

Beyond founding companies, Kramer has been a prolific angel investor, making over 61 investments primarily in cybersecurity and enterprise software [^5][^6]. He was born in 1966 and studied at the Hebrew University of Jerusalem [^2].

## Stated Thesis

*(Self-reported: These represent what Kramer says publicly about his investing approach. See Inferred Thesis for analysis of actual investment behavior.)*

Kramer has stated: "Startups are a very human experience. They're all about taking calculated risks. It's okay to fail as long as you learn as much as you can from that failure, and continue to have real human ambition and dreams" [^3].

On competition, Kramer notes: "If you have a good idea, you should expect that there are 10 other startups that are going to do the same thing. And it's not only about the idea, it's really about how you execute around that idea" [^3].

On the Israeli cybersecurity ecosystem: "I'm super optimistic about the Israel cybersecurity ecosystem" and sees "continued momentum" with "Israeli companies, Israeli startups get lots of investment at very high valuations" [^3].

On reinvention: "If you're lucky to have been successful, you have to make sure you are not frozen in that persona" [^1].

On Cato's vision: "We want to be remembered as the AWS of IT security" [^1].

## Inferred Thesis

The analysis below is based on 21 verified investments by Shlomo Kramer [^5][^6][^7][^8].

**Sector concentration (based on 21 verified investments):**
- Cybersecurity: 12 of 21 (57%) — Palo Alto Networks, Trusteer, Exabeam, SafeBreach, LightCyber, TopSpin Security, Lacoon Mobile Security, Indegy, Cynet, Aqua Security, WatchDox, At-Bay (cyber insurance)
- Enterprise software / SaaS: 4 of 21 (19%) — Gong.io, Sumo Logic, Worklight, Business Layers
- Fintech: 2 of 21 (10%) — Fundbox, At-Bay (also categorized in cyber)
- Data / storage: 2 of 21 (10%) — Excelero Storage, Comilion
- Other: 1 of 21 (5%) — Splacer (marketplace), Insert (mobile)

**Key patterns:**

- **Cybersecurity dominance**: Over half of Kramer's investments (57%) are in cybersecurity, consistent with his identity as the "godfather of Israeli cybersecurity." He brings deep domain expertise and network effects that few other angel investors can match.

- **Extraordinary angel returns**: Kramer wrote the first angel check in Palo Alto Networks and sat on its board; the company is now valued at over $100 billion [^2][^3]. His investment in Trusteer resulted in a $1 billion acquisition by IBM [^2][^6]. These two investments alone represent exceptional returns.

- **Active board participation**: Kramer has served as a board member at Exabeam, Aqua Security, Gong, and At-Bay, indicating deep engagement beyond capital deployment [^5][^6].

- **Israeli ecosystem focus**: The vast majority of Kramer's investments are in Israeli-founded companies, leveraging his unparalleled network in Israel's cybersecurity ecosystem [^3][^5].

- **Seed and Series A concentration**: Kramer typically invests at the earliest stages, often writing the first institutional check. His Series A investment in Gong.io ($20 million in 2017) represents a larger check size than typical angels [^6][^7].

- **Pattern: security category creation**: Kramer's personal companies and investments share a common thread of creating new security categories — firewalls (Check Point), WAF (Imperva), SASE (Cato), container security (Aqua Security), breach simulation (SafeBreach).

- **Non-security bets are enterprise-adjacent**: Even when investing outside cybersecurity, Kramer focuses on enterprise software (Gong, Sumo Logic) rather than consumer companies.
