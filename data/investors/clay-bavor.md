## Background

Clay Bavor is the co-founder of Sierra, a conversational AI platform for businesses, which he founded alongside Bret Taylor in early 2023 [^1]. Bavor and Taylor first met as members of Google's Associate Product Manager (APM) program around 2005 [^2].

Bavor earned a bachelor's degree in computer science from Princeton University [^3]. He spent 18 years at Google (2005–2023), where he rose from associate product manager to Vice President of Product Management [^4]. Early in his Google career, he worked on Search and advertising products, then led the product and design teams for Gmail, Google Drive, Google Docs, and Google Apps for Enterprise (now Google Workspace) [^1] [^4].

In 2015, Bavor started and led Google's AR/VR initiative, overseeing products including Google Cardboard, Daydream, Project Starline, and Google Lens [^4] [^5]. He reported directly to CEO Sundar Pichai and was named to Fortune's 40 Under 40 list in 2016 at age 34 for his work leading Google's VR division [^3]. In November 2021, a Google reorganization created Google Labs — a new division focused on "high-potential, long-term projects" including AR/VR and Area 120 — with Bavor at the helm [^6].

Bavor announced his departure from Google in February 2023. In a LinkedIn post, he wrote that he started his first business in second grade selling Japanese candies and ran a website design company from age 13 through high school, adding: "My time at Google has been one of the great gifts of my life" [^7]. His final day at Google was in March 2023 [^5].

Sierra launched publicly in February 2024 with $110 million in Series A funding co-led by Sequoia Capital and Benchmark [^2]. The company has since raised $635 million total across multiple rounds and reached a $10 billion valuation as of September 2025 [^8]. Sierra hit $100 million in annual recurring revenue just seven quarters after launch [^9].

## Stated Thesis

Clay Bavor is primarily an operator and company-builder rather than an active investor. No public angel investments, advisory board positions, or venture fund participation beyond his role co-founding Sierra have been identified through available sources.

At Sierra, Bavor has articulated a thesis about the future of AI agents in business. He has stated that AI agents represent a new category of software, analogous to previous platform shifts: "In 1995, if you wanted to exist digitally, you needed a website. Ten years ago, you needed a mobile app. Now, you need an AI agent" [^10]. He has described Sierra's goal as helping companies "build their own customer-facing AI agents for all parts of their customer experience," predicting that "your AI agent will be more important than your website" [^11].

Bavor has emphasized that deploying AI in customer-facing contexts requires deep partnership rather than self-service tooling, noting that the approach is to work closely with customers to capture brand voice, business processes, and domain knowledge [^10].

## In Their Own Words

On the power of AI to self-correct: "The solution to many problems with AI is more AI...LLMs are better at detecting errors in their own output than not making those errors" [^10].

On Sierra's vision: "What we help companies do is build their own customer-facing AI agents for all parts of their customer experience. We think in the future, your AI agent will be more important than your website" [^11].

On AI adoption speed: "ChatGPT took 25 months or something to go from not existing to something like 10% of the world using it in a given week" [^11].

On Sierra's approach to enterprise AI: "Most people just want to buy a couch" — describing Sierra's built-for-you model versus self-service DIY tools [^2].

On AI safety in customer-facing contexts: "You obviously don't want an AI that is representing your company, your brand, to your customers making stuff up. It's a problem area that we at Sierra have really run towards, and there's no silver bullet" [^10].

On leaving Google: "My time at Google has been one of the great gifts of my life" [^7].
