## Background

Kevin Systrom was born on December 30, 1983, in Holliston, Massachusetts [^1]. He attended Middlesex School in Concord, Massachusetts, where he was introduced to computer programming [^1]. He graduated from Stanford University in 2006 with a bachelor's degree in management science and engineering [^1]. At Stanford, he was one of twelve students selected for the Mayfield Fellows Program, which provided training in high-tech entrepreneurship [^1]. Through that program, he interned at Odeo, the company that later became Twitter [^1].

After Stanford, Systrom joined Google, working on Gmail, Google Calendar, and other products as a product marketer for two years [^1]. He left Google and in 2010 co-founded Instagram with Mike Krieger in San Francisco [^1]. Meta Platforms (then Facebook) acquired Instagram for approximately $1 billion in 2012, when the company had 13 employees [^1]. Systrom served as CEO for eight years until September 2018, when he resigned [^1].

In January 2023, Systrom and Krieger launched Artifact, an AI-powered news app [^2]. After Systrom announced in January 2024 that the app would shut down, citing insufficient market opportunity [^2], Yahoo acquired Artifact in April 2024 for its AI-powered personalization technology [^3].

Beyond his companies, Systrom has been an active angel investor, deploying personal capital into consumer and enterprise startups [^4].

## Stated Thesis

Systrom publicly describes his investment approach as an extension of his product instincts from building Instagram [^5].

On product-market fit, Systrom has stated he wants to see evidence that people already love the product — he is not interested in ideas that might work someday [^5].

On growth, Systrom looks for organic, word-of-mouth growth rather than paid acquisition, noting that if a product requires huge marketing spend to acquire users, he is not interested [^5].

On founders, Systrom values those who can ship fast, learn from users, and iterate based on real feedback, rather than spending months planning perfect launches [^5].

On product philosophy, Systrom favors products that tap into existing human behavior rather than create new behavior, citing how Instagram succeeded because people already loved taking photos — it just made sharing easier and more beautiful [^5].

On broader philosophy, Systrom has stated: "I think not focusing on money makes you sane, because in the long run it can probably drive you crazy" [^6].

## In Their Own Words

On entrepreneurship: "If you've got an idea, start today. There's no better time than now to get going. That doesn't mean quit your job and jump into your idea 100% from day one, but there's always small progress that can be made to start the movement" [^6].

On product focus: "Don't do too much, and cut away everything that doesn't matter to the success of your product" [^6].

On being right: "I think a big part of knowing that you're right is working as hard as you can to prove that you're wrong. And if you can't, well, there's only one option left, which is: you're probably right" [^11].

On finding talent: "I look for passion. I think smart people are passionate people. When you ask them what they're really good at and what they're really passionate about, usually they can explain it in a way that makes you get inspired about something" [^6].

On learning through doing: "You can't learn about investing unless you invest" [^11].

On shutting down Artifact: Systrom acknowledged that "the market opportunity isn't big enough to warrant continued investment in this way" [^2].
