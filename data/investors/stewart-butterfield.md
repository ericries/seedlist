## Background

Stewart Butterfield (born Dharma Jeremy Butterfield, March 21, 1973) is a Canadian entrepreneur and angel investor best known as the co-founder of Flickr and Slack [^1]. He spent his first five years in a log cabin without running water or electricity in Lund, British Columbia, where his family had moved after his father fled the US to avoid the Vietnam War draft [^1]. At age 12 he legally changed his name to Daniel Stewart Butterfield [^1].

Butterfield earned a B.A. in philosophy from the University of Victoria in 1996, and a Master of Philosophy from Clare College, Cambridge, in 1998, with a focus on philosophy of biology, cognitive science, and philosophy of mind [^1].

In 2000, Butterfield co-founded Gradfinder.com, which was subsequently acquired [^2]. In 2002, he co-founded Ludicorp in Vancouver with Caterina Fake and Jason Classon to build an online multiplayer game called Game Neverending [^1]. As the game struggled to gain traction, a photo-sharing feature inside it became the core of Flickr, which launched in 2004 [^1]. Yahoo acquired Ludicorp in March 2005, and Butterfield continued as General Manager of Flickr until departing Yahoo in July 2008 [^1].

In 2009, Butterfield co-founded Tiny Speck, which launched the massively multiplayer game Glitch in September 2011 [^1]. After Glitch failed to attract a large enough audience and shut down in December 2012, the internal communications tool the team had built to coordinate remote work was spun out as Slack [^1]. Slack's preview release in August 2013 attracted over 120,000 daily users in its first week after the public launch in February 2014 [^2]. In December 2020, Salesforce announced its acquisition of Slack for $27.7 billion, finalizing the deal in 2021 [^1].

Butterfield announced his departure as CEO of Slack in December 2022, with his last day in January 2023 [^3]. After leaving, he has focused on angel investing, family, philanthropy, and personal projects [^4].

## Stated Thesis

Butterfield has not published a formal investment thesis. His public portfolio and rare interview comments suggest he invests primarily where his operator experience is most directly applicable: workplace collaboration, productivity tools, developer infrastructure, AI, and occasionally Canadian fintech [^5].

When he departed Slack, Butterfield stated he planned to step back from entrepreneurship: "I'm not going to do anything entrepreneurial" [^3]. His subsequent investing activity tells a different story — he has made at least 13 publicly verifiable investments since 2016, accelerating after his departure from Salesforce.

In his investment in Vectara (2022), Butterfield offered his clearest public statement on what attracts him to a company: "By applying cutting-edge neural networks, Vectara is bringing much-needed innovation to the challenge of improving product search" [^6]. The statement reflects a pattern: he backs companies solving infrastructure or workflow problems that he encountered firsthand as a product builder.

## In Their Own Words

On neural search and product discovery (Vectara launch, October 2022):

> "By applying cutting-edge neural networks, Vectara is bringing much-needed innovation to the challenge of improving product search." [^6]

On the root cause of tech over-hiring (Bloomberg/Fortune, May 2023):

> "The more people who report to you, the higher your prestige, the more your power in the organization." [^20]

> "Every budgeting process is, 'I really want to hire,' and that to me is the root of all the excess." [^20]

> "Even back then, it was apparent — we would say in interviews, this is just zero interest rate. This is what happens." [^20]

On his departure from Slack and plans (internal Slack message, December 2022):

> "FWIW: This has nothing to do with Bret's departure. Planning has been in the works for several months! Just weird timing." [^3]

On his company Slack's internal philosophy (internal memo to Tiny Speck team, July 31, 2013):

Stewart Butterfield sent a memo to his team two weeks before Slack's preview release articulating that the product was not really about messaging software: "That's why what we're selling is organizational transformation. The software just happens to be the part we're able to build & ship." [^21]

On entrepreneurship and the role of product craft (from the "We Don't Sell Saddles Here" memo, 2013):

> "When you want something really bad, you will put up with a lot of flaws. But if you do not yet know you want something, your tolerance will be much lower." [^21]

On leadership and transparency (Startup Grind interview):

> "It sucks to be the CEO of a startup that's doing super well." [^22]
