## Background

Steve Jurvetson is an American venture capitalist and co-founder of Future Ventures, an early-stage venture firm he launched in 2018 with Maryanna Saenko [^1]. He is one of Silicon Valley's most prominent deep-tech investors, with a career spanning over three decades.

Jurvetson completed his undergraduate degree in Electrical Engineering at Stanford University in 2.5 years, graduating first in his class [^2]. He subsequently earned a Master of Science in Electrical Engineering and an MBA from Stanford Business School, where he was an Arjay Miller Scholar [^2] [^3]. Before entering venture capital, he worked as an R&D engineer at Hewlett-Packard, where seven of his chip designs were fabricated, held product marketing roles at Apple and NeXT, and served as a management consultant at Bain & Company [^2] [^3].

Jurvetson joined Draper Fisher Associates in 1994 after his second year of business school and became a partner [^4]. In 1997, the firm was renamed Draper Fisher Jurvetson (DFJ) [^4]. At DFJ, he led a $300,000 investment in Hotmail in 1996 — a deal that more than 20 other firms had passed on — which returned approximately 1,300x when Microsoft acquired the company for $400 million just 18 months later [^5] [^6]. He is also credited, along with Tim Draper, with coining the term "viral marketing" in 1997, inspired by Hotmail's explosive growth strategy of appending "Get your free e-mail at Hotmail" to every outgoing message [^7].

At DFJ, Jurvetson built a portfolio that included early investments in Baidu, Skype, Tesla, SpaceX, D-Wave, Planet Labs, Nervana Systems, Zoox, and Cyras Systems (acquired for $8 billion) [^4] [^2]. He was a member of the Tesla board of directors from 2006 to December 2020 and has served on the SpaceX board for over 14 years [^8] [^9].

In November 2017, Jurvetson stepped down from DFJ following an internal investigation [^10]. Six months later, in April 2018, he co-founded Future Ventures with Maryanna Saenko, who had previously worked with him at DFJ [^1] [^11].

In 2016, President Barack Obama appointed Jurvetson as a Presidential Ambassador for Global Entrepreneurship [^3]. He has been named to the Forbes Midas List of Tech's Top Investors five times (2011-2014 and 2016) and was named Silicon Valley Venture Capitalist of the Year by Deloitte in 2012 [^12] [^13]. He was also named to the MIT Technology Review Innovators Under 35 in 1999 [^6].

## Stated Thesis

Jurvetson publicly describes himself as "an early-stage venture capitalist with a focus on founder-led, mission-driven companies at the cutting edge of disruptive technology and new industry formation" [^2].

Future Ventures positions itself around the idea that investing in companies that improve the world generates superior returns. Jurvetson has stated: "Focus on change that matters and the money will follow" and "Companies that improve the world in a meaningful way are much more satisfying than those that just made the most money" [^14].

Jurvetson emphasizes contrarian thinking as central to his approach: "Usually, you need to think outside the box to change the world. If everyone you know or the majority of the partners think a new investment is a good idea, it probably isn't" and "Looking back, almost every great idea was regarded as dumb by most VCs" [^15].

He has articulated a deliberate strategy around small fund sizes: "The bigger the fund size the worse the returns. A $1bn fund cannot perform as well as a $50m fund" and maintains that venture capital does not scale, with Future Ventures structured as a two-person partnership by design [^14].

Future Ventures operates with a 15-year fund structure designed for patient capital, and Jurvetson has stated he has never sold a share in any company he holds [^16]. The inaugural $200M fund focused on "environmentally sustainable transportation, food technology and high power computer systems" [^1].

## Inferred Thesis

Based on 28 verified investments across DFJ and Future Ventures (see Portfolio below). Jurvetson's stated thesis aligns reasonably well with his actual behavior, though the portfolio reveals several patterns not emphasized in his public messaging.

**Sector distribution (28 verified investments):**
- Space / aerospace: 5 of 28 (18%) — SpaceX, Planet Labs, Zoox (autonomous), Xona Space Systems, The Boring Company
- AI / deep learning / computing: 5 of 28 (18%) — Nervana Systems, Mythic, D-Wave, xAI, Glass
- Food / agriculture tech: 5 of 28 (18%) — Upside Foods (Memphis Meats), The Better Meat Co, Beeflow, Superplum, Verdant Robotics
- Biotech / therapeutics: 4 of 28 (14%) — Faeth Therapeutics, Moonwalk Biosciences, Sensei Biotherapeutics, Cambrian Bio
- Clean energy / sustainability: 3 of 28 (11%) — Tesla, Commonwealth Fusion Systems, TMC The Metals Company
- Internet / software (historical): 4 of 28 (14%) — Hotmail, Baidu, Skype, MobileCoin
- Neurotechnology: 1 of 28 (4%) — Neuralink
- Other: 1 of 28 (4%) — Interwoven

**Stage distribution:** Jurvetson heavily favors early-stage investing. The majority of his verified investments are seed or Series A, consistent with his stated preference. However, through Future Ventures, he has also participated in larger follow-on rounds for portfolio companies like Upside Foods (Series B) and Faeth Therapeutics.

**Key patterns:**
- **Deep Elon Musk alignment:** Jurvetson has invested in at least 7 Musk-associated companies (Tesla, SpaceX, The Boring Company, Neuralink, xAI, Zoox, and others), representing a unique concentration among major VCs [^17].
- **Hardware and atoms over bits:** Unlike most Silicon Valley VCs, Jurvetson's portfolio skews heavily toward physical-world technology — rockets, quantum computers, cultivated meat, fusion energy, robotics. Pure software plays are mostly historical (pre-2010).
- **Mission-driven filter:** Nearly every investment has an explicit world-improvement thesis (clean energy, sustainable food, space access, disease treatment). This is consistent with his stated thesis.
- **Patient capital orientation:** With 15-year fund structures and his stated policy of never selling shares, Jurvetson's approach accommodates the longer development timelines inherent in deep-tech.
- **Two-person decision-making:** Future Ventures operates with only two partners (Jurvetson and Saenko), which Jurvetson argues leads to better decisions [^14].
- **Geographic concentration:** Portfolio is heavily concentrated in the San Francisco Bay Area, with some investments in Southern California (SpaceX in Hawthorne) and internationally (Baidu in China, D-Wave in Canada).

**Notable gaps:** Despite claiming broad deep-tech interest, there are no verified investments in areas like cybersecurity, traditional SaaS, fintech, or consumer internet. The historical internet investments (Hotmail, Skype, Baidu) predate the Future Ventures era and reflect DFJ's broader mandate.

**Fund sizing:** Future Ventures has maintained consistent $200M fund sizes across three main funds (2019, 2021, 2023) and raised a $169M AI-focused fund in 2024, suggesting deliberate discipline around fund scale [^18] [^19] [^20].

## In Their Own Words

"Usually, you need to think outside the box to change the world. If everyone you know or the majority of the partners think a new investment is a good idea, it probably isn't. Looking back, almost every great idea was regarded as dumb by most VCs." — Steve Jurvetson, VC Lab interview, 2021 [^15]

"The bigger the fund size the worse the returns. A $1bn fund cannot perform as well as a $50m fund. Smaller is better in Venture Capital, but one is not great." — Steve Jurvetson, VC Lab interview, 2023 [^14]

"I think I would be a worse investor if I was alone." — Steve Jurvetson, on his two-person partnership with Maryanna Saenko, VC Lab interview, 2023 [^14]

"We apply a filter to our investments and try to invest in companies that are unlike anything we've seen before." — Steve Jurvetson, Thought Economics interview [^33]

"They are self-confident enough to be humble. They have a reservoir, a personal drive, passion, belief in their capabilities." — Steve Jurvetson, on the traits of great entrepreneurs, Thought Economics interview [^33]

"You need to find entrepreneurs you want to back for life, and not take an activist stance. My job is to be a coach, confidante and mentor." — Steve Jurvetson, Thought Economics interview [^33]

"I was a believer all along, and just wanted my partners to let me invest in something this cool. I really like rockets." — Steve Jurvetson, on investing in SpaceX, Silicon Valley Churchill Club talk [^34]

"SpaceX lowered the cost of going into space by 10x. The ministers of China say, 'We can't compete on price with that.' In how many industries have you heard ministries of China say ever say that?" — Steve Jurvetson, Silicon Valley Churchill Club talk [^34]

"You simply cannot hold out that you are smarter, better or harder-working. You got lucky. If your stance is that you got lucky, it eliminates the 'I know it all' attitude and allows you to make better decisions going forward." — Steve Jurvetson, Thought Economics interview [^33]

"Clean meat is an enormous technological shift for humanity, and an opportunity to invest in something so important does not come along often. This is a moment where the investment potential and the potential to do good for the world are both off the charts." — Steve Jurvetson, on investing in Memphis Meats (now Upside Foods), TechCrunch, August 2017 [^26]

"It's good to be back in the saddle again." — Steve Jurvetson, on launching Future Ventures, TechCrunch, February 2019 [^11]
