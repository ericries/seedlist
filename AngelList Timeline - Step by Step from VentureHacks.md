# AngelList: A Detailed Timeline of Every Step Nivi & Naval Took

*Reconstructed chronologically from every AngelList-related post on VentureHacks*

---

## 2007 — The Foundation

**Step: Launch Venture Hacks blog.**
Nivi and Naval start publishing Venture Hacks, a blog focused on educating founders about fundraising, term sheets, cap tables, and negotiation tactics. They take an explicitly pro-founder stance — telling entrepreneurs things investors don't want them to know.

**What they published:** Posts like "How to make a cap table," "The Option Pool Shuffle," and "How to pick a co-founder" become canonical startup reading. These aren't lightweight listicles — they're deeply tactical, insider guides.

**Why it mattered:** This built a captive audience of founders AND investors who respected their judgment. For three years, they gave away their best thinking for free. By the time they launched a product, they didn't have a cold-start problem — they had a warm, trusting audience on both sides of the market.

---

## 2007–2009 — Building the Rolodex (Quietly)

**Step: Develop deep personal relationships with active angel investors.**
Through the blog, speaking, and direct interactions, Nivi and Naval build personal relationships with dozens of the most active angel investors in Silicon Valley. They understand each investor's preferences, check sizes, deal flow needs, and frustrations.

**Step: Understand the pain on both sides.**
Founders can't find investors. Investors are drowning in low-quality deal flow. Intros happen through a patchwork of personal networks that exclude most founders. There's no centralized, transparent system.

---

## Pre-Launch (Late 2009 / Early 2010) — The "AngelBase" Experiment

**Step: Publish a blog post called "AngelBase" — a curated list of angel investors.**
Before AngelList existed as a separate product, Nivi and Naval published what was essentially a blog post listing angel investors with their profiles and preferences. This was the proto-AngelList — a simple list, published on the Venture Hacks blog.

**Step: Test demand by manually brokering intros.**
Even before the formal launch, they began manually introducing startups to investors, using their personal relationships and judgment to match quality startups with relevant angels. This was entirely human-powered — no platform, no software.

---

## February 2, 2010 — Official Launch

**Step: Publish the launch post — "Launch: AngelList, a curated list of angel investors."**
Nivi publishes the formal announcement on Venture Hacks. Key details:

- ~55 angel investors at launch
- Collectively representing ~$80M in planned investments for 2010
- Each investor has a detailed profile: investment frequency, check sizes, market focus, intro preferences
- Named launch investors include: Jeff Clavier, Dave McClure, Rob Hayes, Aaron Patzer, Brad Feld

**Step: Set strict quality criteria for the supply side (investors).**
To be listed, investors must demonstrate: two investments of $25K+ in 2009, with plans for at least two more in 2010. This is a curated club, not an open directory.

**Step: Set strict expectations for the demand side (startups).**
Founders are told: build an MVP, get customer traction, THEN send a 150-word elevator pitch. Nivi explicitly warns: "Don't send them nonsense. Angels talk to each other and they talk to me. Your reputation is all you've got."

**Step: Position it as information that doesn't exist anywhere else.**
The launch post emphasizes that this data — investor preferences, check sizes, intro protocols — is unavailable anywhere else. The data itself is the product.

---

## February–March 2010 — First Traction

**Step: Personally review every single startup submission.**
Nivi and Naval review every startup that applies. If a startup isn't ready, they contact the founders and suggest profile improvements. If a startup is investment-grade, they personally email it to relevant investors.

**Step: Offer two intro modes — custom and mass.**
- Custom intros: startups pick specific investors → ~50% response rate
- Mass intros: pitch goes to everyone on the list
- Most startups ask for both

**Step: Track and publicize which angels are taking meetings.**
By March 2010, Nivi publishes that 40+ angels have taken meetings through AngelList. Named participants include Dharmesh Shah, Jeff Clavier, Shervin Pishevar, Mark Suster, Keith Rabois, and Bijan Sabet. Getting permission to use their names publicly is itself a form of social proof.

## March 2010 — First Funded Startup

**Step: Announce the first startup funded via AngelList.**
(Referenced in the meetings post as already announced — the specific startup predates the Postling announcement.)

**Step: Publish Postling case study — "$200K raised in 6 days."**
Postling becomes a flagship early success story. Key details published:

- Founder Dave Lifson shares the story at SXSW

**Step: Publish "AngelList is the real deal" — founder testimonials.**
Three founders share endorsements:
- Tony Wright (RescueTime): AngelList bridged the post-Y Combinator gap
- Paul Stamatiou (Skribit): praised the personalized communication even when not selected
- Dave Lifson (Postling): $200K in 6 days during SXSW

## April–June 2010 — The Case Study Drumbeat Begins

**Step: Publish a steady stream of funded-startup case studies.**
Each follows a template: startup name, what they do, who invested through AngelList, what the founder said, plus announcement of new investors joining the platform. Posts include:

- **BlockChalk** — Used AngelList to fill out an existing round. Key insight: "second-order intros" — investor Josh Stylman introduced them to Chris Dixon and Founder Collective, who also invested.
- **MightyMeeting** (NYC) — Got 10 intros in 4 days. Demonstrated AngelList works for non-SF startups. Closed seed round in 21 days.
- **Divvyshot** — Becomes the first AngelList-funded startup acquired (by Facebook). A major milestone for platform credibility.

**Step: Announce new high-profile investors joining the platform.**
Each case study post also includes names of newly joined investors: Mitch Kapor, Ram Shriram, Mike Maples, etc. This creates a virtuous cycle — investors join because other investors are joining.

## July 2010 — Growing Scale

**Step: Publish Thumbtack case study.**
Thumbtack raises $1.2M. Started with 3 angels from traditional networking, added 3 more through AngelList. CEO Marco Zappacosta: "The introductions have been killer, even when they didn't net a check." WSJ picks up the story.

**Step: Earn mainstream media coverage.**
The Wall Street Journal reports that about half of the 48 companies featured on AngelList had received funding. WSJ conclusion: "the service is good at getting worthy start-ups into the inbox of investors."

**Step: Xconomy publishes in-depth analysis.**
Article titled "In Seed Funding Race, AngelList Sorts the 'Junk' from the 'Maybes'" — Naval explains the value proposition: helping investors find deal flow, simplify syndication, and match to their interests.

---

## August–October 2010 — Building Platform Features

**Step: Publish LearnBoost case study.**
Demonstrates the "Power Broker" concept — Harper Reed referred LearnBoost to AngelList, connecting them with George Zachary and Jeff Fagnan, who then introduced them to more investors. Shows the network effect in action.

**Step: Crowdsource success stories on Quora.**
Rather than writing case studies themselves, Nivi creates a Quora thread: "Which startups have been funded via AngelList?" By October 2010, 27 startups have self-reported, with the real number estimated at ~40.

**Step: Launch real-time activity feed on the homepage.**
Shows anonymized investor actions in real time — intros, investments, shares, likes. Startup names are hidden. Investor anonymity is optional. Serves dual purpose: shows the platform is alive and active, and gives investors a way to be visible on the homepage (which they want).

**Step: Launch the AngelList Blog.**
Moves from announcing features on Venture Hacks to having a dedicated AngelList blog. Key philosophy: "don't expect polished posts — we share works in progress that are already shipped." Transparency as a brand attribute.

---

## Late 2010 — The Scouts Program

**Step: Launch AngelList Scouts.**
Formalize the referral network with a Scouts program:
- Trusted entrepreneurs and investors can refer startups directly to AngelList under their own names
- Scouts get profile badges showing referral track records
- Startup names appear on Scout profiles (with permission)
- Barrier to entry: refer one high-quality startup
- Initial scouts from Silicon Valley, LA, Seattle, Chicago, NYC, Boston, and London

**Why it mattered:** Scaled deal flow without hiring. Turned power users into active promoters. Created status incentives within the ecosystem.

## Early 2011 — Expansion to VCs and Series A

**Step: Announce "Raise a Series A on AngelList."**
Platform now includes "almost 300 VCs" — Sequoia, Kleiner Perkins, Accel, Greylock. Four startups have completed Series A rounds through the platform.

**Step: Publish data showing VCs are more active than angels.**
AllThingsD publishes data Nivi provides: 725 angels vs. 557 VCs on the platform, but VCs are slightly more engaged (5.6 intros on average for seed funds, 5.4 for multi-stage VCs, 5.0 for angels). Platform has facilitated connections for 219 startups involving 384 investors.

**Step: Publish the AppMakr case study — "54.5% of the $1M we raised."**
AppMakr attributes more than half their $1M raise directly to AngelList intros. Daniel Odio documents the journey in a series including an interview with Naval.

## March 2011 — Going Global

**Step: Launch location-based matching.**
Investors and startups can now be matched by geography — continents, countries, regions, cities. San Francisco and New York are essentially tied for top startup concentration.

**Step: Articulate the global vision.**
Two effects: "flattening" (investors in India finding startups in New York) and "fattening" (building local ecosystems — startups in Paris finding investors in Paris). Vision: "Silicon Valleys all over the world."

**Step: Publish data on who's actually investing.**
Named VC firms sourcing deals through AngelList: Kleiner Perkins, Matrix Partners, CRV, Google Ventures, Atlas, Greylock, Softbank, Floodgate, General Catalyst. Angel investors include Matt Mullenweg, Mitch Kapor, Dave Morin. Portfolio companies include Uber, Yipit, Wanderfly, Branchout.

**Step: Mark Suster interviews Naval on "This Week in Venture Capital."**
Mainstream VC media is now covering AngelList as a significant force.

## Mid-2011 — Market Matching System

**Step: Launch Markets feature.**
Startups add up to 4 market categories to their profiles. Investors follow or block specific markets. Admins email investors startups in their followed markets before everyone else.

- Markets structured as a directed acyclic graph (not a simple tree) — "Games" can have parents in both "Information Technology" and "Consumers"
- Markets with fewer than 10 followers remain hidden (prevents spam)
- Similar market names auto-consolidated via aliasing
- Market taxonomy derived from investor self-reporting — "the most accurate set of venture capital market names in the world"

## Late 2011 — Taulia and Series A Validation

**Step: Publish Taulia case study — Series A from top-performing VC fund.**
Taulia raises $3M Series A from Matrix Partners through AngelList:
- Posted on a Friday evening
- By end of weekend: ~40 requests, majority from senior partners at top VCs
- "More than doubled the round size and significantly increased the pre-money"
- This proves AngelList works for institutional fundraising, not just angel rounds

## 2012 — Addressing the Critics

**Step: Publish "Venture Hacks sucks now, all you talk about is AngelList."**
Nivi addresses reader complaints directly. His response: "AngelList is product-izing everything we've written on Venture Hacks." The blog isn't marketing for the product — the product is the natural extension of the blog. Office motto: "productize yourself."

**Step: Publish "Why would a seasoned entrepreneur use AngelList?"**
Three reasons articulated:
1. Market terms — pitching multiple investors simultaneously reveals true market value
2. Access to value-add investors beyond your existing network — Fred Wilson notes AngelList "adds people to the mix that would not be part of the offline deal sharing networks"
3. Speed — "Within hours of posting, we had dozens of qualified investors and by end of day were 100% oversubscribed"

---

## 2013 — Culture and Philosophy

**Step: Publish the operating philosophy.**
A series of posts codify how AngelList operates internally:

- **"No email at AngelList"** — Replace email with specialized tools: Yammer for company-wide async, HipChat for sync chat, Tracker for project management, face-to-face for high-bandwidth communication. Claims 90% reduction in email.

- **"Ask forgiveness, not permission"** — Extreme employee autonomy.

- **"1-(wo)man startups"** — Each person operates as a one-person startup within the company.

- **"6-year vesting"** — Longer vesting than industry standard, aligned with long-term thinking.

- **"Things we care about"** — Six principles: reducing barriers for startups, efficient connections, investor tools, honest communication, scalable (product-based) solutions over manual labor, team autonomy.

- **"The Entrepreneurial Age"** — Naval's thesis that the future belongs to those who pursue quality and scale simultaneously. "Quality without scale is not entrepreneurship — it is a tree falling in the forest with no one around."

**Step: Publish the new employee reading list.**
Three categories:
- Culture: their own essays on values
- Execution: internal docs on customer service, engineering, reviews
- External influences: Netflix culture deck, Yishan Wong on engineering management, Paul English on hiring

**Step: Hire by publishing the job as content.**
When recruiting a product designer, Nivi publishes the process as a blog post — describing exactly what they're looking for (a "product designer" who can build what they design), asking the community for referrals, and requiring only a few portfolio links to apply.

## Late 2013 — Formal Separation

**Step: Venture Hacks becomes independent of AngelList.**
Nivi formally separates the two. In the announcement, he notes that "the first version of AngelList actually originated as a blog post on Venture Hacks, originally called AngelBase." The blog moves to WordPress hosting. The product and the content platform have fully diverged.

2. **Publish a curated list as a blog post** (late 2009)

4. **Manually broker every transaction with personal judgment** (Feb 2010 onward)
5. **Publish a case study for every single success** (Mar 2010 onward)
6. **Earn mainstream media through results, not pitching** (Jul 2010)
7. **Launch a Scouts program to scale deal flow through power users** (late 2010)
8. **Expand from angels to VCs / Series A** (early 2011)
9. **Add market and geographic matching** (2011)
10. **Publish comprehensive metrics to prove scale** (Aug 2011)
11. **Codify operating philosophy to attract missionaries** (2012–2013)
12. **Separate the content brand from the product brand** (late 2013)
